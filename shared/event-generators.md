# Event Generators

Dramatic mechanics that transform static character profiles into concrete, escalating events. These generators synthesize McKee (GAP, Risk, Reversals), Truby (Self-Revelation, Opponent), Marks (Fatal Flaw, Midpoint), Chamberlain (CATCH, FLAW-to-STRENGTH), and Hutzler (Fears, Dark Side, Life Lessons).

**When to use:** Character phase (as building blocks within character files) and structure phase (as input for arcs and plot).

**Integration principle:** Every generator takes CHARACTER INPUT (from the derivation chain) and produces STORY OUTPUT (concrete events that can be sequenced).

---

## 1. GAP Generator (McKee)

**Source:** Robert McKee, *Story* — the gap between expectation and result is the atom of drama.

### Formula

```
EXPECTATION: Character takes action expecting result X
    -> ACTION: The specific thing they do
        -> REACTION: The world responds — but NOT with X
            -> GAP: The distance between expected and actual result
                -> ESCALATION: Next action must be BIGGER (more risk, more cost)
```

### Escalation Rule

Each successive GAP must widen. The character's actions become more extreme because moderate actions have failed. The cost of each attempt rises. The point of no return approaches.

**Escalation sequence:** Ask -> Demand -> Manipulate -> Coerce -> Destroy

### Training Example: Walter White (Breaking Bad, S1-S2)

| Beat | Expectation | Action | Reaction | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I'll cook once and pay for treatment" | Cooks with Jesse | Tuco won't pay | Money gone, deeper in |
| 2 | "I'll negotiate with Tuco" | Brings product personally | Tuco beats Jesse, threatens both | Violence entered the equation |
| 3 | "I'll scare Tuco into respect" | Brings fulminated mercury, blows up office | Tuco respects him — and now OWNS him | Got what he wanted, lost what he needed (safety) |

Each GAP is wider. Each action is more extreme. Each cost is higher. Walt's Lie ("I am doing this for my family") becomes harder to maintain with each gap.

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Identify 3-5 potential GAP scenarios per protagonist based on their Want and Lie.
- **Structure phase:** Sequence GAPs into escalating episode or act beats.

## 2. CATCH Generator (Chamberlain)

**Source:** Jill Chamberlain, *The Nutshell Technique* — the CATCH is the moment the character gets what they want and discovers the price.

### Formula

```
SETUP WANT: Character pursues conscious goal
    -> POINT OF NO RETURN: Commits fully (bridges burned)
        -> CATCH: Gets the Want — and discovers the hidden cost
            -> FLAW TEST: Must choose between clinging to the Flaw or changing
```

### Escalation Rule

The CATCH must be BUILT INTO the Want. It is not an external punishment. The Want itself contains the trap. The character's Lie made the trap invisible.

### Training Example: Kendall Roy (Succession, S1)

```
SETUP WANT: Kendall wants to take over Waystar from his father. He believes
he deserves it and that running the company will prove he is not the weak
son Logan sees.

POINT OF NO RETURN: The vote of no confidence. Kendall publicly moves
against his father. There is no undoing this. He is either king or exile.

CATCH: Kendall wins the board vote — but Logan outmaneuvers him legally.
Kendall gets the title and discovers it is hollow. He has the chair but not
the power. And now he has made an enemy of the only person whose approval
he actually wanted.

FLAW TEST: Kendall's flaw (seeking validation from the person who withholds
it) is exposed. The CATCH is not that he failed — it's that success tastes
like failure because his Want was a proxy for his Need (his father's love).
```

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Define the CATCH scenario embedded in each protagonist's Want.
- **Structure phase:** The CATCH typically falls at or near the midpoint.

## 3. Fatal Flaw Generator (Marks)

**Source:** Dara Marks, *Inside Story* — the Fatal Flaw is not a weakness but an EXHAUSTED STRENGTH.

### Formula

```
ORIGIN: The Flaw began as a survival strategy (connected to Ghost)
    -> EXHAUSTION: The strategy no longer works but the character cannot see this
        -> CLINGING REFLEX: Under pressure, the character doubles down on the Flaw
            -> FATAL PROGNOSIS: If unchanged, where does this end?
```

### Escalation Rule

The Flaw must have been USEFUL once. It solved a real problem. The tragedy is that it has become the problem — and the character cannot distinguish the tool from themselves.

### Training Example: Elizabeth Jennings (The Americans)

```
ORIGIN: Elizabeth survived the Soviet system by compartmentalizing emotion.
Feeling nothing was how you stayed alive. This is not a flaw — in her
world, it was the skill that kept her breathing.

EXHAUSTION: In America, with children, with Philip, the compartmentalization
is destroying the things she tells herself she is protecting. She cannot
feel for her children without risking her cover. So she feels nothing —
and her children sense the void.

CLINGING REFLEX: When the mission threatens her family, Elizabeth does not
open up — she doubles down. More control. More walls. She handles her
daughter like an asset to manage, not a person to love.

FATAL PROGNOSIS: If unchanged, Elizabeth will complete every mission
perfectly and lose every person who matters. She will win the Cold War
and sit in an empty apartment in Moscow.
```

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Map the Fatal Flaw trajectory for each protagonist.
- **Structure phase:** The Clinging Reflex drives second-act escalation. The Fatal Prognosis is what the climax must address (or confirm).

## 4. Self-Revelation Generator (Truby)

**Source:** John Truby, *The Anatomy of Story* — Self-Revelation requires BOTH psychological and moral recognition, then PROOF through action.

### Formula

```
PSYCHOLOGICAL RECOGNITION: Character sees their own Lie
    + MORAL RECOGNITION: Character sees the harm their Lie caused others
        -> PROOF THROUGH ACTION: Character acts DIFFERENTLY under the same pressure
            -> COST: The new action requires sacrificing the Want
```

### Escalation Rule

Recognition without action is sentimentality. The character must PROVE the revelation by doing the thing they could not do before — and it must COST them the thing they wanted most.

**Critical:** Not every character gets a Self-Revelation. Some characters fail. Some see the truth and choose the Lie anyway. This is a tool, not a requirement.

### Training Example: Philip Jennings (The Americans, Finale)

```
PSYCHOLOGICAL RECOGNITION: Philip sees that his entire American life —
the marriage, the travel agency, the suburban existence — was built on
the same lie as his spy life. He was performing in both.

MORAL RECOGNITION: He sees what the double life cost his children.
Not abstractly. Concretely. Paige standing on the platform. Henry
not knowing.

PROOF THROUGH ACTION: Philip chooses to leave. Not heroically. Not
with a speech. He gets on the train. He goes back to a country he
barely knows. The proof is in the leaving — he gives up the Want
(American life, family intact) for the Need (honesty, even if it
means loss).

COST: His children. There is no reunion scene. No phone call that
makes it okay. The cost is permanent.
```

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Plan whether and when Self-Revelation occurs (or fails to occur).
- **Structure phase:** Self-Revelation belongs in the climactic sequence — never before.

## 5. Dark Side Escalation Ladder (Hutzler + McKee)

**Source:** Enneagram-based Fears and Dark Side patterns (Hutzler), escalation dynamics (McKee). The Dark Side is not a single trait — it is a TRAJECTORY with five stages.

### Formula

```
STAGE 1 — PRESSURE: External threat activates the character's core Fear
    -> STAGE 2 — FIRST SLIP: Character uses a Dark Side behavior that "works"
        -> STAGE 3 — NORMALIZATION: The behavior becomes a tool, used more freely
            -> STAGE 4 — ESCALATION: The behavior intensifies, collateral damage grows
                -> STAGE 5 — POINT OF NO RETURN: The behavior defines the character
```

Each stage has: **Pressure** (what triggers it) / **Action** (what they do) / **Cost** (what it destroys) / **Next Pressure** (why it escalates)

### Training Example: Jimmy McGill -> Saul Goodman (Better Call Saul)

| Stage | Pressure | Action | Cost | Next Pressure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Chuck's contempt, bar hearing | Small con (manipulates elderly client situation) | Kim's trust, slightly eroded | Needs to prove himself WITHOUT Chuck |
| 2 | Financial desperation, pride | The billboard stunt — manufactures publicity | Professional reputation among peers | Kim distances herself |
| 3 | Losing Kim's respect | Increasingly elaborate schemes become his METHOD | Cannot distinguish Jimmy from Slippin' Jimmy | Legal consequences approach |
| 4 | Legal exposure, Howard as obstacle | Destroys Howard's reputation systematically | Howard's life. Kim's soul. His own. | Howard is dead in his apartment |
| 5 | Loss of everything real | Becomes Saul Goodman completely — the mask is the face | Jimmy McGill. Gone. | There is no next pressure. There is only Saul. |

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Map all 5 stages for each character with Dark Side potential.
- **Structure phase:** Distribute stages across the season/act structure. Stage 1 = early. Stage 5 = climax or finale.

## 6. Collision Beat Generator (Truby + McKee)

**Source:** Truby's opponent design + McKee's scene dynamics. A Collision Beat is what happens when two characters with INCOMPATIBLE METHODS pursue a SHARED GOAL — or when their methods are compatible but their goals are mutually exclusive.

### Formula

```
SHARED ELEMENT: What binds these two characters (goal, person, space, resource)
    + INCOMPATIBLE METHOD: How each pursues/protects the shared element
        -> PRESSURE: External force that makes coexistence impossible
            -> BEAT 1: First collision — each holds position
                -> BEAT 2: Escalation — one gains advantage
                    -> BEAT 3: Reversal or rupture
```

### Training Example: Shiv Roy & Tom Wambsgans (Succession)

```
SHARED ELEMENT: The marriage — which is also a power alliance.

INCOMPATIBLE METHOD: Shiv uses the marriage as a platform (she needs
Tom loyal, visible, and manageable). Tom uses the marriage as a ladder
(he needs Shiv invested, not just tolerant). Both need the marriage.
Neither needs the same marriage.

PRESSURE: Logan dangles DOJ cooperation. Someone will go to prison.

BEAT 1: Shiv assures Tom she will protect him. Tom believes her
because he needs to. (Collision deferred — tension stored.)

BEAT 2: Shiv makes a deal that exposes Tom. Not maliciously — she
simply did not factor him in. Tom realizes he was never in the
equation. (Collision visible.)

BEAT 3: Tom calls Logan. The reversal is total. The person Shiv
controlled becomes the person who ends her. (Collision detonates.)
```

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Identify 2-3 collision pairs per ensemble and map their shared element + incompatible methods.
- **Structure phase:** Collision beats are scene-level building blocks. Distribute across episodes/acts with escalating intensity.

## 7. Dangling Cause Generator (McKee)

**Source:** McKee's principle that great narrative plants causes early and detonates them late. The audience forgets. The story doesn't.

### Formula

```
PLANTING: Establish a fact, object, relationship, or capability EARLY
    -> SLEEP: The planted element recedes from attention (2-5 scenes/episodes minimum)
        -> IGNITION: A new event reactivates the planted element
            -> DETONATION: The planted element changes everything
```

### Escalation Rule

The gap between planting and detonation determines the power of the payoff. Too short = predictable. Too long = forgotten. The sweet spot: the audience has stopped thinking about it but REMEMBERS it instantly when it detonates.

### Training Example: Chekhov's Ricin (Breaking Bad)

```
PLANTING (S2): Walt and Jesse make ricin to kill Tuco. The ricin is
established as a thing that exists, a capability Walt has.

SLEEP (S2-S4): The ricin comes up occasionally but never detonates
as intended. It becomes a background element. The audience knows
it exists but stops expecting it.

IGNITION (S4): The ricin cigarette goes missing. Jesse believes
Gus poisoned Brock with it. Walt uses this belief to turn Jesse
against Gus.

DETONATION (S5): Jesse realizes Walt orchestrated the ricin
disappearance. The planted element — now three seasons old —
detonates the Walt-Jesse relationship permanently.
```

### Training Example: The Photograph (The Americans)

```
PLANTING (S1): The FBI has a blurry photograph that might be
Philip and Elizabeth. It exists. It is filed.

SLEEP (S1-S5): The photograph threat recedes. Other dangers
take priority. The audience forgets.

IGNITION (S5): A new sketch artist, a new witness, a new
technology. The photograph comes back.

DETONATION (S6): The drawing based on the photograph is in
every field office. Philip and Elizabeth's faces are on a
board in Stan Beeman's office. The thing planted in Season 1
ends the series.
```

### Integration

- **Character phase:** Identify 2-3 elements per character that can be planted early (objects, relationships, secrets, capabilities).
- **Structure phase:** Map planting/sleep/ignition/detonation across the full arc. Minimum 2 dangling causes per season (TV) or per act (film). Games: dangling causes across branching paths create the illusion of a world that remembers.

## Integration Map

| Generator | Character Phase Use | Structure Phase Use |
|---|---|---|
| GAP | Define 3-5 potential gap scenarios per protagonist | Sequence into escalating episode/act beats |
| CATCH | Define the trap embedded in each Want | Place at/near midpoint |
| Fatal Flaw | Map the flaw trajectory (origin to prognosis) | Drive second-act escalation |
| Self-Revelation | Plan if/when revelation occurs | Place in climactic sequence |
| Dark Side Ladder | Map all 5 stages per character | Distribute across full arc |
| Collision Beat | Identify pairs and incompatible methods | Build scene-level confrontations |
| Dangling Cause | Identify plantable elements | Map planting/detonation across full arc |

**Rule:** Every protagonist should have material from at least generators 1, 2, 3, and 5. Generators 4, 6, and 7 are used selectively based on the story's needs.
