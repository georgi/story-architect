# 05 WORLD — Sub-Worlds Method

## Purpose

Build the narrative world from character needs outward. The world is not a setting — it is a dramatic instrument. Every location exists because it forces characters to confront their Lie, embody the Value Conflict, or collide with each other.

---

## Prerequisites

- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/theme.md` — Value Conflict and facets
- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/characters/*.md` — Character files (at least Ghost, Lie, Want, Need)
- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/research/` — Research material

---

## Step 1: Define the Overworld

Where, When, What conditions. 3–5 sentences maximum.

- **Where:** Geographic location, scope (a city, a building, a region)
- **When:** Time period, season, era
- **What conditions:** The rules of this world — economic, social, political, physical

The overworld is the container. It establishes what is possible and what is not. It does NOT need to be exotic — a suburb can be as dramatic as a war zone.

---

## Step 2: Brainstorm Sub-Worlds from Characters

For EACH character, ask:
- Where does their **Need** manifest? (What environment reflects their deficit?)
- Where does their **Ghost** echo? (What place mirrors or inverts their formative experience?)
- Where is their **daily life**? (Where do they spend time, work, exist?)
- Where does their **Want** take them? (What places does their goal require?)

List ALL possible sub-worlds. Don't filter yet. Cast a wide net.

A sub-world is a distinct environment with its own:
- Physical characteristics (looks, sounds, smells)
- Social rules (who has power, what's taboo)
- Emotional register (how it feels to be there)

---

## Step 3: Select 4–8 Sub-Worlds

Filter the brainstorm list through three filters. A sub-world must pass at least 2 of 3:

### Theme Filter
Does this sub-world embody or challenge the Value Conflict? Can you point to the specific value it serves or undermines?

### Tension Filter
Does this sub-world create dramatic tension for at least 2 characters? Not just "they're both there" — it must generate friction, opposition, or forced choices.

### Friction Filter
Does this sub-world force at least one character to confront their Lie? Being in this place must make it harder to maintain the false belief.

**Target: 4–8 sub-worlds.** Fewer than 4 = world too narrow. More than 8 = world unfocused.

---

## Step 4: Character-Subworld Assignment

For EACH character, define:

### Main Sub-World
Where they spend most of their time. This is their home territory — where they have (or believe they have) control.

### Friction Test
How does this sub-world challenge the character's Lie? What about this environment makes the Lie harder to sustain?

### Ghost Echo
How does this sub-world mirror or invert the Ghost? Does it recreate the conditions of the formative experience? Does it represent the escape from it?

### Location Function
What dramatic purpose does this specific place serve? Every sub-world must DO something to the characters who inhabit it — not just house them.

---

## Step 5: Boundaries Between Sub-Worlds

For every pair of adjacent sub-worlds, define:

- **What happens when characters cross from one to another?** Physical, social, and emotional transitions.
- **What is the cost of crossing?** What do you lose, risk, or reveal by entering the other world?
- **Power dynamics at borders:** Who controls access? Who is welcome and who is not? What are the gatekeeping mechanisms?

Boundaries are where drama concentrates. A character from Sub-World A entering Sub-World B is inherently dramatic — if the boundary is meaningful.

---

## Step 6: Collision Locations

Every **Collision Pair** (⚡) from the ensemble MUST have a physical place where they collide. Not abstract — a specific location with sensory detail.

For each Collision Pair:
- **Where do they collide?** Name the specific location.
- **Why HERE?** What about this place makes the collision inevitable or intensified?
- **Sensory detail:** What does this location look, sound, smell like during the collision?
- **Power dynamic:** Who has the advantage in this location? Does it shift?

If a Collision Pair has no physical location for their conflict, the world is incomplete.

---

## Step 7: Conflict World

Identify the single most dramatic location — the **Conflict World**. This is where the Value Conflict reaches its highest intensity. Where the climax happens.

- **What is this place?** Describe it concretely.
- **Why THIS place?** What about it concentrates the Value Conflict?
- **Who controls it?** And who wants to?
- **What are its rules?** What is possible here that isn't possible elsewhere?
- **Sensory signature:** What is the defining sensory experience of this place?

The Conflict World is not just a backdrop for the climax — it IS the climax, externalized as geography.

---

## Quality Gate

Before completing world building, verify:

- [ ] 4–8 sub-worlds (not fewer, not more)
- [ ] Every sub-world derived from character needs (not invented for variety)
- [ ] Every sub-world passes at least 2 of 3 filters (theme, tension, friction)
- [ ] Every character assigned to a main sub-world with friction test, ghost echo, and location function
- [ ] World rules defined: what is IMPOSSIBLE in this world (constraints create drama)
- [ ] Boundaries between adjacent sub-worlds defined with power dynamics
- [ ] Collision locations for ALL Collision Pairs — specific, sensory, not abstract
- [ ] Conflict World identified — the single most dramatic location
- [ ] Concreteness Mandate: every location has physical detail, not just thematic function

---

## Output

File: `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/world.md`

Sections:
1. Overworld (Where, When, What conditions)
2. Sub-Worlds (4-8, each with description, filters passed, rules)
3. Character-Subworld Map (per character: main sub-world, friction test, ghost echo, function)
4. Boundaries (per adjacent pair: transition, cost, power dynamics)
5. Collision Locations (per Collision Pair: where, why, sensory detail, power dynamic)
6. Conflict World (the climactic location, fully described)
