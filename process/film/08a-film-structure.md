# 08a FILM STRUCTURE — Sequence-Based Architecture (Film)

## Purpose

Build the film's dramatic structure using the sequence approach (Gulino) combined with McKee's value charges and Truby's moral argument. Every structural decision driven by character psychology and Value Conflict.

## Eight Sequences Across Three Acts

### Act 1: Setup (Sequences A-B, ~25% of runtime)

#### Sequence A: The World Before
- Establish the protagonist's status quo — their Here and Now
- Show the Lie functioning (barely) — the coping strategy that still works
- Introduce the world rules and the protagonist's place in it
- Plant at least one key relationship and one secret
- **Value charge at end of A:** Protagonist's dominant value appears stable

#### Sequence B: The Disruption
- The inciting incident — an event that ACTIVATES the protagonist's Want
- The protagonist makes a decision to pursue the Want (commitment)
- First glimpse of the opposing value — someone or something challenges the Lie
- Establish the central dramatic question of the film
- **Value charge at end of B:** Positive shift (the pursuit feels possible)

### Act 2: Confrontation (Sequences C-F, ~50% of runtime)

#### Sequence C: First Attempts
- Protagonist pursues Want with initial strategies
- Early successes that reinforce the Lie ("this is working")
- Introduction of the primary opponent — someone who embodies the antithesis facet
- First CATCH: a strategy backfires, creating a new problem
- **Value charge at end of C:** Positive but with cracks

#### Sequence D: Complications
- Strategies become more desperate
- Alliances form and strain
- Crucible begins operating — Want pursuit visibly worsens Need deficit
- Sub-worlds expand — protagonist enters unfamiliar territory
- **Value charge at end of D:** Turning negative — the cost of pursuit becomes visible

#### Sequence E: Midpoint
- **Greatest external success** — the protagonist achieves something significant
- **Greatest internal loss** — simultaneously, something essential is lost or betrayed
- The Midpoint is a REVERSAL: what looked like progress was actually the deepest trap
- The protagonist glimpses their Need for the first time (without recognizing it)
- **Value charge at end of E:** Apparent positive masking deep negative

#### Sequence F: Consequences
- Midpoint fallout — the internal loss catches up
- Dark Side behavior escalates (Dark Side Ladder steps 2-3)
- Key relationships fracture
- The opponent tightens pressure
- The protagonist's Lie is publicly or privately exposed (but not yet recognized)
- **Value charge at end of F:** Strongly negative — all strategies failing

### Act 3: Resolution (Sequences G-H, ~25% of runtime)

#### Sequence G: Crisis
- Lowest point — all external strategies exhausted
- The protagonist must confront the Lie directly
- Self-Revelation moment: the character sees what they've been doing (accepts or rejects)
- Dark Side Ladder peak — or the beginning of reversal
- **Value charge at end of G:** Maximum negative — the question is whether anything can be salvaged

#### Sequence H: Climax + Resolution
- **The Climactic Choice:** The protagonist must choose between Want and Need
- This choice is the step from Fatal Flaw to strength (or the final descent)
- The choice physically takes place in the Conflict World
- Consequences cascade through the ensemble
- Resolution shows the new status quo — transformed (or destroyed)
- **Value charge at end of H:** Final state — embodies the Message

## Midpoint Design

The Midpoint is the structural fulcrum. It must satisfy BOTH conditions:

### Greatest External Success
- What does the protagonist achieve?
- Why does this feel like a victory?
- Who celebrates or acknowledges it?

### Greatest Internal Loss
- What is lost simultaneously?
- Does the protagonist know it's lost? (Often: no)
- When will the loss become visible? (Sequence F)

### The Trap
- How does the success mask the loss?
- What decision at the midpoint locks the protagonist into the second half?
- Why can't they go back?

---

## Climactic Choice

The final, defining decision. The Climactic Choice must be:

- **A step from Fatal Flaw to strength** (positive arc) OR **a final embrace of the Flaw** (negative arc)
- **Binary:** Two options, both with real consequences. Not "right vs. wrong" but "Value A vs. Value B"
- **Irreversible:** The world after this choice is permanently different
- **Public:** Other characters witness or are affected by the choice
- **Physical:** Takes place in a specific location (Conflict World) with visible action

---

## Ensemble and Opponent Mapping

### Opponent
The primary opponent is not a villain — they are the strongest embodiment of the antithesis facet. They believe they are RIGHT, and in some ways, they ARE right.

- What value does the opponent embody?
- Where is the opponent genuinely correct?
- How does the opponent mirror the protagonist (shared trait, different response)?
- How does the opponent force the protagonist to confront the Lie?

### Supporting Ensemble
For each supporting character:
- Which sequences are they primarily active in?
- What is their function in the protagonist's arc? (Catalyst, mirror, obstacle, ally)
- When does their relationship with the protagonist shift?

---

## Theme Through Structure

The Value Conflict must be visible in the STRUCTURE, not just the content:

- **Act 1:** Value A appears correct. The world seems to reward it.
- **Act 2 first half:** Value A's limits emerge. Value B gains validity.
- **Midpoint:** Both values collide. Neither wins.
- **Act 2 second half:** The cost of choosing one value over the other becomes devastating.
- **Act 3:** The protagonist's choice resolves the Value Conflict — through action, not words.

---

## Quality Gate

Before completing film structure, verify:

- [ ] 8 sequences defined with value charges
- [ ] Protagonist arc mapped across all sequences
- [ ] Midpoint satisfies BOTH conditions (external success + internal loss)
- [ ] Climactic Choice is binary, irreversible, public, physical
- [ ] Climactic Choice embodies Message through action
- [ ] All event building blocks (GAP, CATCH, Fatal Flaw, Self-Revelation, Dark Side Ladder) placed in specific sequences
- [ ] Opponent is a facet embodiment, not a generic antagonist
- [ ] Ensemble members mapped to sequences with function defined
- [ ] Value Conflict visible in structural progression (not just dialogue)
- [ ] Conflict World is the location of the climax
- [ ] No sequence exists without a character decision driving it
