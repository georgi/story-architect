# 06 TONE — Tonality Definition

## Purpose

Define HOW the narrative feels — its emotional register, dialogue style, pacing, and aesthetic. Tonality is synthesized from three sources, not chosen from a menu.

---

## Prerequisites

- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/theme.md` — Value Conflict
- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/characters/*.md` — Character files
- `wiki/projects/[PROJECT]/world.md` — Sub-worlds and Conflict World
- `wiki/author-voice.md` — Author's established voice

---

## Three Sources

Tonality is not invented — it is derived from three sources:

### Source 1: Genre Impulse from Value Conflict
What emotional register does this specific Value Conflict DEMAND?

- Loyalty vs. Justice → moral thriller, weight, consequence
- Truth vs. Mercy → intimate drama, quiet devastation
- Safety vs. Freedom → paranoia, claustrophobia, then explosion

The Value Conflict dictates the genre feel. Not the other way around.

### Source 2: Character Voices from Ensemble
How do THESE specific people speak? What speech registers exist in this ensemble?

- Educational level, profession, region
- Subtext capacity: who says what they mean? Who never does?
- Silence patterns: who goes quiet when threatened? Who escalates verbally?
- Humor: who uses it as weapon? As shield? As bridge?

### Source 3: Atmosphere from Conflict World
What does THIS world look, sound, feel like?

- Light quality, color palette, textures
- Ambient sound, silence, noise patterns
- Temperature, weather, physical sensations
- Time of day patterns — when does drama happen here?

---

## Five Areas to Define

### 1. Narrative Tone

The overall emotional register of the storytelling:
- **Serious / Ironic / Absurd?** Where on this spectrum?
- **Warm / Cold?** How much emotional proximity to characters?
- **Intimate / Epic?** Close-up or wide shot as default mode?
- **Grounded / Heightened?** Naturalism or stylization?

State it directly. Then give an example scene that demonstrates the tone.

### 2. Dialogue Style

How characters speak:
- **Realistic / Heightened?** Everyday speech or crafted dialogue?
- **Subtext-heavy / Direct?** How much lies beneath the surface?
- **Register range:** How far apart are the speech registers in the ensemble?
- **Signature patterns:** Any recurring verbal habits, rhythms, or structural rules?

Give 2-3 example exchanges that demonstrate the dialogue style.

### 3. Pacing

How time moves in the narrative:
- **Fast / Slow?** Default tempo.
- **Even / Rhythmic?** Constant pace or deliberate variation?
- **Where does it breathe?** Which scenes/types get space and silence?
- **Where does it sprint?** Which scenes/types compress and accelerate?
- **Tension architecture:** How is tension built and released? Sharp cuts or slow builds?

### 4. Visual Language

The aesthetic dimension:

**For screen (TV/Film):**
- Colors, textures, lighting style
- Camera movement patterns (handheld, steady, tracking)
- Editing rhythm (long takes, quick cuts, match cuts)
- Visual motifs that recur

**For games:**
- Art style and aesthetic direction
- UI philosophy (diegetic, minimal, immersive)
- Environmental storytelling approach

**For text/prose:**
- Sentence length and rhythm
- POV and tense
- Descriptive density
- Metaphor register

### 5. Reference Matrix

Position the work in relation to existing works:

**Format:** "X meets Y, but without Z."

Rules:
- NOT the obvious genre reference (if it's a crime series, don't say "like The Wire")
- At least one reference from OUTSIDE the expected genre
- Explain what each reference contributes: tone from X, structure from Y, character approach from Z
- Be specific: not "Breaking Bad" but "Breaking Bad's slow-burn domestic scenes, not its action sequences"

Provide 3-5 reference points with specific contribution.

---

## Pitch Tone

One sentence that captures the feeling of the entire narrative. Must match the author's voice from `wiki/author-voice.md`.

This sentence goes into the pitch. It is not a logline — it is an emotional promise.

---

## Quality Gate

Before completing tonality, verify:

- [ ] Synthesized from all 3 sources (genre impulse, character voices, atmosphere) — not just one
- [ ] Genre named without compromise — not hedging with "elements of"
- [ ] Atmosphere is SENSORY, not generic ("cold fluorescent light" not "tense atmosphere")
- [ ] Dialogue style demonstrated with example exchanges
- [ ] Reference Matrix distinctive — not the obvious comparisons
- [ ] Pitch Tone present — one sentence, matches author voice
- [ ] Ruthlessness Check: Is the tone UNCOMFORTABLE enough for this material? If it feels safe, it's wrong.
- [ ] Concreteness Mandate: every tonal description evokes a specific sensation
