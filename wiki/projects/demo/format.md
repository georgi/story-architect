# Format — The Iron Pass

## Medium
Television Series — Streaming / Premium Cable

---

## Structure

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Season Count** | 1 season (limited series) — multi-season potential conditional on arc resolution |
| **Episode Count** | 6 episodes |
| **Runtime per Episode** | 55–65 minutes (variable; content-paced, no hard cap) |
| **Serialization Degree** | Fully serialized — horizontal narration, no standalone episodes |
| **Season Shape** | Slow compression: the pass narrows, the snow thickens, the room to maneuver disappears |
| **A/B/C Story Assignment** | See breakdown below |
| **Act Structure per Episode** | 5 acts, no cold open (exception: Episode 1) |
| **Cold Open Strategy** | Episode 1 only — one image, no dialogue, no context |
| **Cliffhanger Strategy** | Threshold cliffhangers: a character crosses a line they cannot uncross |

---

## Justification

### From Pacing

The tone document specifies slow compression — "the pass narrows, the snow thickens, then a gate opens and what's outside is worse than the cold." This is not a series of incidents. It is a single sustained squeeze. Slow compression dies in 8 or 10 episodes: the middle sags, padding enters, and the atmospheric pressure dissipates. Six episodes keeps the walls moving at all times. You feel the mountain closing in from Episode 2 onward because there is nowhere to put filler.

The 5/4 time signature described in tone — always slightly off-balance — is a structural as well as musical metaphor. Five acts per episode generates that rhythm: scenes resolve into new instability rather than traditional act-break cliffhangers. The viewer never quite lands.

Variable runtime (55–65 minutes) rather than a fixed clock respects the dialogue style: spare, transactional, weighted by silence. Scenes that run on silence cannot be trimmed to fit a clock without destroying their function. The content determines when the episode ends.

### From Ensemble

Two POV characters. One Collision Pair. This is a chamber piece. The format options for two-character dynamics are: film (too compressed for two Dark Side Ladders of five stages each), limited series (correct), or novella (wrong medium for a world this visual).

Six episodes allocates roughly three episodes per character's dominant arc phase while keeping them in constant collision. Serana's Dark Side Ladder runs five stages — Vigilance, Testing, Leverage, Isolation, The Siege. Aldric's runs five stages — Over-documentation, Verification loops, Accusatory pattern recognition, Isolation through distrust, The Violation. Ten arc stages across two characters across six episodes means approximately 1.5 to 2 stages advance per episode. That is the right pace: visible movement without rushed transformation.

Eight episodes would require satellite content — invented secondary characters with their own arcs — to fill the runtime. That satellite content would dilute the claustrophobia. The fortress works because there is nowhere else to look.

### From Scope

Three sub-worlds: Ironhold, the Hall of Ledgers, the Black Vein. All three are contained within the same physical structure — the fortress and the mountain beneath it. This is not an expanded narrative. It is an intensely local one. The world is small by design; the pressure comes from density, not size.

Cross-sub-world contamination is already built in: Serana's power in Ironhold bleeds into the Hall of Ledgers through doctored entries; her Ghost lives in the Black Vein. Aldric's only safe jurisdiction (the Hall of Ledgers) is surrounded by Serana's reality on all sides. This contamination does not require additional episodes to establish — it needs episodes in which the contamination is experienced and escalated.

Six episodes matches the scope. A limited series is the correct container for a story in which the location is the antagonist.

### From Theme

Control vs. Trust requires time to develop ambivalence — both values must be shown as genuinely protective before they are shown as genuinely destructive. If the format forces early resolution, the theme collapses into a verdict. The audience must spend enough time inside Serana's control to feel why it works before they feel why it corrodes. Same for Aldric's proceduralism: it must feel like rigor before it reveals itself as terror.

Six episodes at 55–65 minutes each gives approximately six hours of story time. That is enough to make Control feel like a reasonable response to a world that cannot be trusted, and to make Trust feel like the only thing holding civilization together — and then to demonstrate that both characters are building the thing that will destroy them. A 90-minute film cannot hold that ambivalence. It forces a verdict. Six episodes does not.

The Value Conflict also requires that neither character arrives at a clear Life Lesson within the season. The message — power seized to protect becomes indistinguishable from the tyranny it replaced — must land through action and consequence, not through a character recognizing their Lie. Season 1 ends with both characters having descended one stage further than they intended, not with resolution. The format must allow the theme to remain open.

## A/B/C Story Structure

### Default Assignment

| Story Slot | Function | Content |
|------------|----------|---------|
| **A-Story** | The investigation — Aldric's Want | Each episode, Aldric pursues the case through proper channels. The A-story advances the plot (new evidence, new obstacle, new procedural move). This is the forward momentum that makes the episode feel like it's going somewhere. |
| **B-Story** | The fortress under pressure — Serana's Want | Serana responds to Aldric's investigation by tightening her grip. The B-story shows the mechanism of her control: a captain tested, a ledger sealed, a family warned. This is where her Dark Side stages manifest as concrete action. |
| **C-Story** | The sub-world beneath — the Black Vein | The miners, Brynn the barkeep, the collective memory of the pass. The C-story holds the thematic substrate: what Control and Trust look like when they're not mediated by power. It also functions as pressure gauge — the miners' mood tells us how close to breaking the system is. |

### Note on Collision

The A and B stories are not parallel — they are in constant collision. Aldric's A-story move at the end of an act triggers Serana's B-story response at the start of the next. The plots are not juxtaposed; they are cause-and-effect within the same episode. This is what makes six episodes feel sufficient: the collision pair does the work of an ensemble twice its size.

The C-story (the Black Vein) intersects the A/B collision once per episode — a single scene in which either Aldric or Serana enters the miners' world and is measured against it. This scene is always the episode's emotional low point or revelation: not a plot beat, but a moment of exposure.

## Act Structure per Episode

Five acts. No hard time breaks — act divisions are defined by turning points, not clock positions.

| Act | Function |
|-----|----------|
| **Act 1** | Status quo of the episode — where do the characters stand after last episode's threshold? Establish the episode's A-story problem. |
| **Act 2** | First move — Aldric advances (or attempts to advance) the investigation. Serana responds. The collision begins. |
| **Act 3** | Complication — one character's move has unexpected consequences. The C-story intersects. Exposure moment. |
| **Act 4** | Escalation — both characters double down. Dark Side stages deepen. The pass narrows. |
| **Act 5** | Threshold — one character crosses a line. The episode ends on the consequence of that crossing, not on suspense about what will happen next. |

The five-act structure generates the 5/4 rhythm: Act 3 (the complication) is always the shortest and most destabilizing act. It lands in the wrong place rhythmically — earlier than expected, leaving Acts 4 and 5 to reckon with it. The viewer never quite catches their breath before the threshold.

## Cold Open Strategy

**Episode 1 only.**

One image, no dialogue, no context. The courtyard of Ironhold at dawn. A twelve-year-old girl standing at the edge of the frame. An axe rising. The episode title appears before the sound arrives.

This establishes the Ghost before the character — the viewer carries it into every scene with Serana before they know who she is. It functions as the series' thesis image: power exercised without witness. Everything that follows is a consequence of that morning.

Episodes 2–6 open in the middle of an action, mid-conversation, mid-consequence of the previous episode's threshold. No recap. No previously on. The viewer is expected to remember because the series is built to be remembered.

## Cliffhanger / Episode-End Strategy

The cliffhanger term as classically defined — action suspended at peak tension — is wrong for this material. The tone document is explicit: silence between words carries more weight than the words. An action cliffhanger would betray the register.

**Threshold cliffhangers instead.** Each episode ends on the moment AFTER a character has crossed a line — not on the suspense of whether they will cross it, but on the first breath of the world in which they already have.

The difference:
- Action cliffhanger: Aldric's door is kicked in as the screen cuts to black. (Will he survive?)
- Threshold cliffhanger: Aldric sits alone at his desk. He writes one false word into a witness statement. He sets down the pen. The screen holds on his hands.

The question at the end of each episode is not "what happens next?" — it is "who is this person now?" That is a harder question to stop thinking about.

**Episode-specific threshold logic:**

| Episode | Threshold Character | Threshold |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Aldric | He realizes the legal channels are compromised. He chooses to file anyway. |
| 2 | Serana | She engineers a test for her captains. One passes. She promotes him. He doesn't know he passed by betraying someone. |
| 3 | Aldric | He rides to confirm testimony he already confirmed. He finds it holds. He doesn't trust it. |
| 4 | Serana | She dismisses her inner council. Eats alone. The fortress still functions. She does not understand yet that it functions from fear. |
| 5 | Aldric | He writes the false witness statement. |
| 6 | Serana | She closes the pass. |

## Episode / Arc Sketch (Season 1)

This is not a plot outline — it is a structural map showing how the content fits the container.

**Episodes 1–2: Establishment of the pressure cooker.**
Aldric arrives. The pass is introduced through his Southern eyes — the cold, the stone, the fortress that doesn't want him. Serana's control is shown functioning: tolls collected, miners managed, merchants processed. The A-story begins (missing tribute, doctored ledgers). Serana's Dark Side at Stage 1 (Vigilance) and Stage 2 (Testing). Aldric's Dark Side at Stage 1 (Over-documentation). The Collision Pair establishes its methodology. First threshold: both characters reveal how they operate under pressure.

**Episodes 3–4: The contamination becomes visible.**
Aldric discovers that the legal channels (the Hall of Ledgers) are compromised — the magistrates below have been bought. His proceduralism begins to buckle: he cannot advance the case by the rules because the rules have been captured. Serana moves to Stage 3 (Leverage) — she is now collecting debts, not just controlling movement. The Black Vein appears as a location in which both characters are exposed to what they are each protecting and destroying. A miner's complaint about tunnel supports surfaces — echo of Serana's father's death. She handles it the way Lord Harren would have. She doesn't see it.

**Episodes 5–6: The Siege.**
Aldric fabricates the witness statement (his Stage 5 violation). He has become Oren. Serana closes the pass (her Stage 5 siege). She has become Lord Harren. The collision pair reaches its maximum pressure point: two people who came to the mountain to do the right thing have each become the thing they were sent to stop. The season ends without resolution. The pass is closed. The kingdom begins to feel it. Neither character has recognized their Lie. The message lands through consequence, not confession.

## Season Potential

**Limited series by design. Conditional second season.**

Season 1 ends with the pass closed and both characters at the bottom of their Dark Side Ladders. This is not a cliffhanger ending — it is a consequence landing. The story of Season 1 is complete: the fortress has destroyed the people who tried to hold it with one hand.

A second season would require that the consequences of the closed pass arrive: the kingdom starves, a new authority is sent, and one or both characters must operate in a world where their darkest version is now the only version on record. The thematic territory shifts from "how do you become the thing you fought?" to "who are you when everyone already knows what you are?" That is a different story, and it would need to be earned.

Do not greenlight Season 2 until Season 1 is locked. The limited series frame is not a hedge — it is the structurally honest container for this material.

## Risks

**Risk 1: Two-character weight in six hours.**
Six hours is a long time with only one Collision Pair. The C-story (the Black Vein, the miners) must carry genuine dramatic weight — not just atmosphere. If the C-story remains a mood palette, episodes will feel static in their second act. The miners need at least one character (Brynn, or one of the mining families) who functions as a third pressure point — not a POV character with their own arc, but a recurring presence who measures the two leads against what they were supposed to protect.

**Risk 2: Slow compression requires a visible ratchet.**
The pacing works if the audience feels the pass narrowing episode by episode. If any episode fails to advance a Dark Side stage for at least one character, the compression reads as stasis. Every episode must end with the fortress slightly smaller than it was at the start. This is a discipline problem — the writers' room must resist "breathing room" episodes that reset rather than escalate.

**Risk 3: Threshold cliffhangers require audience trust.**
The choice not to use action cliffhangers is correct for the material and a genuine risk for retention. Threshold cliffhangers work when the audience is deeply invested in who the character is becoming. If the first two episodes fail to establish that investment — if Serana and Aldric remain positions rather than people — the episode-end strategy will feel like a series that forgets to have endings. The cold open of Episode 1 and the first Black Vein scene must do the heavy lifting of making these people feel irreplaceable before the threshold strategy can pay off.

**Risk 4: The season cannot end on hope it hasn't earned.**
The tone document is explicit: "hope is earned, never given." The Season 1 ending — both characters at their darkest stage, the pass closed — must resist any closing image that offers comfort. No last-minute recognition of the Lie, no gesture toward redemption, no score that says things will be okay. The mountain does not care. The ending must honor that.
