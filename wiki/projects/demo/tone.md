# Tone — The Iron Pass

## Narrative Tone
Protokollton. The narrator lists what happened and who was there. Emotion arrives through behavior — knuckles around a ledger, a toll gate closed three hours early, a barkeep who stops refilling a cup. The Iron Pass runs on transactions: iron for grain, grain for silence, silence for survival. When that system skips a beat, everyone in the fortress feels it before anyone says a word.

## Dialogue Style
- **Default Mode:** Functional. People say what they need, nothing adjacent to it. In the Black Vein a miner asks for another round by tapping the cup. Serana authorizes a shipment in eleven words. Aldric logs a discrepancy and waits.
- **Pressure Mode:** Shorter. One extra sentence — the sentence that shouldn't have been said — does the damage. Everything after that is cleanup.
- **Intimacy Mode:** Side by side, not face to face. Two people at the same fire, watching the same pass. What doesn't get said is the content.
- **Forbidden Register:** Confession. Nobody in Ironhold names what they feel. Serana doesn't say she's afraid the snows will trap Aldric inside the fortress long enough for him to find what he's looking for. She closes the east gate two days early and calls it weather.

## Pacing
- **Scene Tempo:** Cut fast between locations. Serana signs a toll order — cut to the caravan captain watching the gate. Aldric copies a ledger entry — cut to Serana watching him from the upper hall. No establishing breath. The mountain doesn't wait.
- **Escalation Pattern:** Accumulation of small transactions that can't be reversed. Not a single explosion — a series of gates closing. Each one reasonable. The pattern only visible in retrospect.
- **Breathing Room:** The mines at shift change. The sound of hammers stopping. A hundred men surfacing in silence, moving to the Black Vein, ordering in sequence. The pause is not peaceful — it's the pause before a count that might not add up.
- **Time Signature:** The pass controls time. Wagons move on Serana's schedule. The ledgers close when she says. The snows set the outer limit and they're coming early. Every scene is slightly behind where it should be.

## Visual/Sensory Language
Iron-gray and soot-black. Torch orange inside, white-gray outside where the snow starts. The camera doesn't drift — it holds position and waits for people to move through the frame. In Ironhold: every shot framed by stone. In the Hall of Ledgers: candles guttering in drafts, shelves of scrolls that reach above the top of the frame. In the Black Vein: low ceilings, the obsidian seam overhead catching firelight, no shot that includes the ceiling and the faces at the same time.

Sound: hammers underneath everything — even when the mines are closed for the night, the mountain settles and the sound is close enough. Wind through arrow slits. Spilled ale on slate. The scratch of Aldric's pen in the ledger room. What you don't hear: music that tells you how to feel.

Prose sentence priority: what the hands are doing, what the room costs to heat, how many men are on the gate, how many days until the pass closes. Adjectives earn their place or they don't appear. "The Black Vein smells like tallow and old iron and someone else's shift" — not "the atmospheric underground tavern."

## Reference Matrix
**Core Formula:** "The Wire in a medieval fortress, with the paranoid institutional claustrophobia of Das Schloss, but without Kafka's abstraction — every rule here has a name attached to it."

### Touchpoints
1. The Wire (seasons 1-3) — specifically: how institutional systems function through the people enforcing them, and how both sides of a power conflict are right about exactly the wrong things. The way McNulty and Stringer Bell are mirrors is the Serana/Aldric template.
2. Deadwood — specifically: a frontier settlement where law and extralegal power are negotiating the same territory simultaneously, and the negotiation IS the drama. The texture of commerce as violence.
3. Das Schloss (Kafka, 1926) — specifically: a closed system where authority is always one door away and the harder you press toward it the more it recedes. But here the castle is concrete — stone walls, ledger entries, specific tolls on specific wagons.
4. Gomorra (the series, not the film) — specifically: the procedural texture of how a parallel power structure actually administers itself day-to-day. The banality of control. The way loyalty is practical before it is emotional.
5. Winter Sleep (Nuri Bilge Ceylan) — specifically: a man of principle trapped with people who see his principles as aggression. The way two people can be completely right and completely destroy each other.

### The Original Move
Every other fortress narrative gives you the fortress as backdrop for something larger — battles, politics, betrayal. Here the fortress IS the argument. The pass controls food. The tolls control the pass. The ledgers control the tolls. Serana controls the ledgers. Aldric's job is to read what she wrote and find the gap between what the ledger says and what the wagons carried. The entire conflict lives in the difference between those two numbers — and both characters know it, and neither can say so directly. The series is an institutional audit conducted between two people who cannot be honest with each other.

## Pitch Tone
A Crown magistrate arrives to audit a mountain fortress and discovers the woman who controls it is doing exactly what the Crown would do — and she knows he knows, and he knows she knows, and the snows are coming in six weeks.
