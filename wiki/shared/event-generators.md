# Event Generators — 7 Dramatic Mechanics for Concrete Events

Tools for agents who need to INVENT specific scenes, behaviors, or actions. Not theory — operational mechanics. Use when a unit needs concretization.

---

## 1. Status Transaction

**Source:** Keith Johnstone via Schuette

Every conversation is shaped by the **played status** of the participants — not social status, but the status each person claims relative to the other in this moment.

### Mechanics

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **High status** | Controls the scene. Steady gaze, slow speech, still body, occupies space. |
| **Low status** | Cedes control. Broken gaze, fast speech, fidgeting, "uhh" at sentence starts. |
| **Status switch** | The moment control shifts within a scene = turning point. |
| **Status contradiction** | Played status contradicts social status (subordinate takes high status) = maximum tension. |

### How to Generate Events

1. Define who STARTS with high status and who with low.
2. Determine the DESIRED status at scene end.
3. Invent the specific moment where status switches — this IS the event.
4. Bonus: Make the switch invisible to one character but visible to the audience (Dramatic Irony).

### Application

- Two characters need a collision → give them the SAME desired status.
- A character needs to reveal their Dark Side → have them seize high status through the specific behavior their Dark Side produces.
- A scene feels flat → add a status reversal mid-scene.

## 2. Emotional Toolbox Mechanics

**Source:** Hutzler (Emotional Toolbox) + Puglisi (Emotion Thesaurus)

### The Fear Engine

Every character is driven by a Core Fear (from their Enneagram type). This fear operates through four mechanisms in EVERY scene:

| Mechanism | Function | Event Type |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **DRIVES** | Fear pushes the character to act | Character does something BECAUSE of the fear |
| **BLOCKS** | Fear prevents the character from acting | Character avoids something BECAUSE of the fear |
| **DISTORTS** | Character masks, manages, compensates | Character hides fear through opposite behavior |
| **ESCALATES** | Antagonist weaponizes the fear | Antagonist triggers fear-based self-destruction |

### How to Generate Events

1. Pick one mechanism (drives/blocks/distorts/escalates).
2. Define the situation that activates it.
3. Write the character's BEHAVIORAL response — using Puglisi's five channels:
   - What they SAY (dialogue — most controlled, most dishonest)
   - How they SOUND (vocal cues — partially controlled)
   - What their BODY does (body language — partially controlled)
   - What they THINK (internal — POV only)
   - What their BODY does involuntarily (visceral — uncontrollable, most honest)
4. The gap between what they SAY and what their BODY does involuntarily = subtext = the event's dramatic power.

### Emotional Sensitivities (Soft Spots)

Emotions the character avoids at all costs because they connect to the Ghost. When triggered: fight/flight/freeze response. No explanation needed — the BEHAVIOR shows everything.

**Template:** [Character] encounters [trigger linked to Ghost]. Without flashback or exposition, show the behavioral cascade: the hot eyes, the cold weight in the chest, the exit strategy.

## 3. Nutshell Technique Application

**Source:** Jill Chamberlain

### The CATCH Mechanic

At the Point of No Return (~25%), the character gets what they WANT — plus something they DIDN'T want (the CATCH). The CATCH is the perfect test of the character's FLAW.

| Element | Function | Test |
|---------|----------|------|
| **SET-UP WANT** | What the character wants in their first dialogue appearance | Established? |
| **POINT OF NO RETURN** | Character gets Want + CATCH | CATCH tests FLAW? |
| **FLAW** | The central character defect | CATCH is perfect test? |
| **CRISIS (comedy) / TRIUMPH (tragedy)** | ~75%: Exact OPPOSITE of Set-Up Want | Inversion verified? |
| **CLIMACTIC CHOICE** | Beginning of Act 3: step away from Flaw (comedy) or deeper into Flaw (tragedy) | Direction clear? |
| **FINAL STEP** | Last scene: further toward Strength (comedy) or further failure (tragedy) | Arc completed? |

### How to Generate Events

1. Define the FLAW as one word/phrase.
2. Define the STRENGTH as its exact opposite.
3. Invent a CATCH that perfectly tests the FLAW — the situation where this specific flaw will cause maximum damage.
4. Build the CRISIS as the exact mirror of the SET-UP WANT: if the character wanted X at the start, at 75% they have the precise opposite of X.

### Chamberlain's Situation Test

> Replace the protagonist with a different character. Does the story still work?
> If yes → it's a situation, not a story. The CATCH doesn't test THIS character's FLAW.

## 4. The GAP — Expectation vs. Result

**Source:** McKee

### Mechanics

When a character acts, they expect a result. The world responds differently. A GAP opens between expectation and outcome. The character must react with GREATER risk, opening a second GAP. This escalation drives story forward.

### The GAP Sequence (4 Steps)

| Step | What Happens | Risk Level |
|------|-------------|------------|
| 1 | Character takes conservative action, expects easy result | Low |
| 2 | World responds unexpectedly. Character must try harder | Medium |
| 3 | Second attempt fails worse. Stakes rise. Character commits more | High |
| 4 | Maximum-risk action. Character is fully exposed | Maximum |

### How to Generate Events

1. Define what the character EXPECTS to happen.
2. Invent the OPPOSITE or PERPENDICULAR outcome.
3. Force the character to respond with greater personal risk.
4. Repeat with escalation until the character is fully exposed.

### Three Registers of the GAP

| Register | GAP produces... | Medium |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| **Comedy** | Laughter — the surprise is absurd | Gap between expected dignity and actual chaos |
| **Tragedy** | Horror — the surprise is devastating | Gap between expected safety and actual destruction |
| **Drama** | Insight — the surprise reveals truth | Gap between expected self and actual self |

## 5. Dilemma Construction

**Source:** McKee (Crisis), Chamberlain (Climactic Choice), Hutzler (Fears)

### Two Types

| Type | Structure | Effect |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Best Bad Choice** | Two options, both harmful. Pick the less terrible one. | Reveals priorities — what the character will sacrifice |
| **Irreconcilable Goods** | Two options, both valuable. Can only have one. | Reveals deepest value — what the character won't surrender |

### Construction Rules

1. **Both options must be genuine.** If one option is obviously right, it's not a dilemma.
2. **The choice must be irreversible.** No take-backs.
3. **The choice must COST something.** The price reveals character.
4. **The choice must connect to Want vs. Need.** The dilemma forces the character to choose between them.
5. **Time pressure.** Remove the luxury of deliberation.

### The Emotional Squeeze (Miller)

At ~75% (end of Act 2), the character is emotionally crushed between two bad options. Their DECISION shows they have changed (comedy) or haven't (tragedy). This is the most character-revealing moment in the story.

### How to Generate Events

1. Identify the character's two most important values (one from Want, one from Need).
2. Create a situation where both are at stake but only one can be saved.
3. Add time pressure.
4. The choice the character makes IS the event. The consequences follow.

## 6. Reversal Mechanics

**Source:** McKee (Gap), Scene Craft (Umkehrprinzip), Calvisi (Signpost Beats)

### The Reversal Principle

Every beat has a three-act structure. Act 2 shows the OPPOSITE of Act 3.

| Desired Outcome | Act 2 Shows | Act 3 |
|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| Character WINS | Apparent FAILURE | Succeeds after all |
| Character LOSES | Apparent SUCCESS | Fails after all |

### Why It Works

The reversal creates: tension (will they make it?), surprise (different than expected), emotional impact (relief or disappointment).

### Types of Reversals

| Type | Mechanic |
|------|----------|
| **Reversal** | Situation flips to its opposite. Character thinks they've won → everything collapses. |
| **Reveal** | Hidden information surfaces. Audience or character gets knowledge that reframes everything. |
| **Complication** | New obstacle or factor. The simple solution becomes unusable. |
| **Midpoint Reversal** | Greatest external success mirrors greatest internal loss (or vice versa). In *Tootsie*: career peak, but living a lie. |

### How to Generate Events

1. Decide the ENDPOINT of the beat first.
2. Write Act 2 as the OPPOSITE of that endpoint.
3. The MOMENT of reversal — the pivot — is the event.
4. Ensure the reversal is surprising but LOGICAL (the audience should think "I didn't see it coming but now it's obvious").

## 7. Pressure / Crucible Intensification

**Source:** Hutzler (Dark Side Ladder), McKee (progressive complication), Marks (Todesschwelle)

### The Dark Side Ladder

5 escalating pressure stages that push the character progressively toward their Dark Side.

| Step | Pressure | Character Response | What the Audience Sees |
|------|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Low** — mild frustration of Want | Coping mechanisms (Lie-based strategies) | Mask intact, charm visible |
| 2 | **Medium** — Want seriously threatened | Coping cracks, first fear-based decisions | First glimpse of what's underneath |
| 3 | **High** — Want appears lost | Abandons normal strategies, desperate measures | Behavior shifts visibly |
| 4 | **Severe** — Fear fully activated | Acts against own values, hurts allies | Audience recognizes the Dark Side |
| 5 | **Maximum** — complete system breakdown | Becomes the worst version of themselves | Full Dark Side — or the moment of choice |

### Marks' Death Threshold Test

> Where will this character be in 5-10 years if nothing changes?

If the answer isn't "death" (physical or metaphorical), the pressure isn't enough.

### Progressive Complication (McKee)

Each scene must show the character MORE exposed, more personally endangered than the last. Cumulative impact. In *Wall Street*: each illegal action makes Fox more vulnerable until he asks "Who am I?"

### How to Generate Events

1. Define the character's current step on the Dark Side Ladder.
2. Invent a pressure event that pushes them to the NEXT step.
3. The pressure event must target the specific FEAR (not generic adversity).
4. Show the behavioral shift through concrete actions — what they DO differently at step 3 that they wouldn't have done at step 1.
5. Each pressure event must have Need costs — pursuing the Want under pressure makes the Need deficit worse.

### Pressure Beat Sequence (for Collision Pairs)

For two characters whose Wants sabotage each other's Needs:

| Pressure | Character A reacts | Character B reacts | What audience SEES |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| First confrontation | ... | ... | Masks intact |
| Stronger pressure | ... | ... | Cracks visible |
| Maximum pressure | ... | ... | True nature revealed |

Fill in the concrete actions for each cell. Three to five pressure stages per collision pair across the season.
